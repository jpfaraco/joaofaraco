How I design products

I've worked in both very individualistic environments and very collaborative ones, and I understand that we need to strike a balance between working alone and bringing in the team to collaborate.

Each product asks for a different process of what basically boils down to (1) understanding the situation, (2) mapping what can be done to solve the complications, (3) figuring out how to solve the complications and (4) validating how effective the solution is.

  1. Understanding a situation means taking internal and external inputs and investigating why things happen. This may range from being a personal itch to something we see users complaining about. Many times capturing these inputs means doing exploratory studies by talking to people, and seeing how they use the product. My job as a designer is to conduct this discovery process and lay these insights out in a way that makes sense for the team to start seeing patterns, formulating hypotheses and ideating on how we can validate the hypotheses.

    üîéÔ∏è Product discovery methods and tools

    The quarantine has definitely shaken things up lately. Not only have I learned to work remotely with the team, but also to keep in touch with our customers. Doing 1:1 exploratory interviews, looking at behavioral and attitudinal feedback and mapping the user journey always helps to give clarity over what we should focus on. For this I'm usually hopping between Miro, Airtable and Google Sheets.

  2. I'm not a big fan of ideation sessions that require people to get together and come up with a lot of ideas in a short period of time. I've seen that most times good ideas come when you're more relaxed and not under too much time or group pressure. Ideas can come at any time, at any place, so I usually give everyone this time to reflect on the insights and think about what can be done, before consolidating with the rest of the team.
  3. Ideas are usually prioritized and then we start sketching out how things should work more specifically, in the form of UI flows, rough wireframes or actual prototypes. For UI flows, Ryan's shorthand has become the standard. When prototyping, I prefer to go high fidelity whenever possible, so that we avoid abstracting important details and can get good quality feedback before development.

    üß∞Ô∏è Design tools

    Photoshop never made sense for interaction design. In fact, very few design tools model how interfaces are actually built. But Photoshop is probably the worst, and I have never used it for such. Today I use a combination of pen and paper, Miro, Figma, HTML/CSS, Protopie and a low-code tool called Bubble to express my designs. I'm always eager to learn new tools, and am not afraid to open the Terminal and bang out some code if needed.

  4. This is where the feedback loops happen. They may be fast or slow, depending on how we're validating the design, and it may happen while designing or in production. Either way, this is probably what got me addicted to digital product design in the first place ‚Äì¬†being able to quickly iterate on designs, experiment new solutions, learn new stuff along the way and see that we can constantly make people's lives better. These feedback loops are Michael Caine.
